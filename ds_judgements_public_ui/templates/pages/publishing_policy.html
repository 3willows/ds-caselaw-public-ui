Publishing Policy for Judgments and Decisions published on Find Case Law

Background

  1. Court Judgments and Tribunal Decisions are important public records.
  2. The National Archives receives selected Court Judgments and Tribunal Decisions for permanent preservation and publication on the Find Case Law service. Currently we receive judgments from the Privy Council, the UK Supreme Court, the Court of Appeal, The High Court, the Upper Tribunals and The Employment Appeal Tribunal. We also receive decisions from some first-tier tribunal chambers and some judgments from the family court.
  3. The National Archives works in partnership with the Ministry of Justice, HMCTS, and the Judicial Office.
  4. The National Archives receives judgments from the Courts and Tribunals through the Transfer Digital Records service (TDR).
  5. The National Archives publishes judgments on the Find Case Law service, which is available to the public at {% translate "caselaw.url" %}.
  6. From 19 April 2022 judgments can be transferred to The National Archives by the court, straight away, for permanent preservation and immediate publication.

Open justice

  1. Open justice is a fundamental principle at the heart of the justice system. It is part of the administration of justice and vital to the rule of law. Justice must not only be done, but it must also be seen to be done. The importance and history of open justice as part of our law can be traced back to before Magna Carta.
  2. Therefore it is important that significant Court Judgments and Tribunal Decisions are available to the public. We publish judgments we receive from the courts and tribunals on the Find Case Law service. It is up to the court to decide whether there is a reason to withhold a judgment from publication.

Personal data

  1. Judgments contain information about people. This typically includes the names of parties and other people mentioned in the judgment, as well as the representatives, and the names of the judges hearing the case. The information about people varies and can be very extensive. It can include a variety of types of special category personal data, depending on the subject matter of the case. The details given in a judgment about the facts of a case may sometimes constitute distressing content (particularly in criminal cases, or cases involving children).
  2. The Court that decided the case is the Data Controller for the publication of judgments on the Find Case Law service. The National Archives is a Data Processor.

    The National Archives cannot and will not takedown a published judgment without explicit instruction from the court. Revisions to judgments already published must also come directly from the court. To contact a specific court or tribunal please see: https://www.gov.uk/find-court-tribunal.
  3. The National Archives publishes selected judgments at the behest of the relevant Courts, who are acting in their judicial capacity. Therefore, this activity is exempt from the provisions of Data Protection Law that would normally apply to the publication of personal data.

Transfer and preservation

  1. The National Archives should only receive a publication version of the judgment. That means any redactions or anonymisation needed for publication, should already have been made by the court.
  2. The National Archives requires that clerks and judges use the Transfer Digital Records (TDR) service to pass the publication version of the judgment to The National Archives.
  3. Judges (those without clerks) can email a Judicial Office Helpdesk for support. The Helpdesk may also use TDR to transfer judgments to The National Archives.
  4. The transfer process has been made as easy as possible for the clerks and judges. The clerk logs in, uploads the document, and receives a reference number. They are then emailed confirmation when the judgment is published.
  5. There are some controls and checks as part of the TDR process. The publication version of the judgment must be a Word document. The TDR service creates a checksum of the judgment on the clerk’s computer, transmits the judgment, and runs various checks on receipt. These include virus scanning, file format signature identification, as well as creating another checksum and compares the two. TDR creates an audit trail that provides the chain of custody for the judgment from the court to The National Archives.
  6. The Word file The National Archives receives through TDR is the digital record of the publication version of the judgment.
  7. In accordance with The Public Records Act 1958 (PRA), The National Archives also permanently preserves the digital record in the Digital Records Infrastructure and creates digital surrogates of the record, for publication and re-use. Our choice of surrogate formats is driven by what is most suitable for the three main types of access we provide. For reading judgments on the Find Case Law website, we use HTML 5. For re-using the judgments as data via our API, we provide LegalDocML. This is an international XML standard for legal documents, also used for legislation. For download and printing a copy of a judgment, we provide a PDF.

Creating digital surrogates

  1. The National Archives will never change the digital record (the Word document we receive from the court).
  2. The National Archives will never change the text of the digital surrogate (i.e., we will not undertake redactions).
  3. The digital surrogate will retain styling and formatting information extracted from the digital record. This enables The National Archives to present the judgment in HTML with reasonable fidelity to the original record.
  4. The National Archives will create and adapt the mark-up of the digital surrogate and adapt the styling and layout in the HTML. This is to make the content as accessible as possible to people using the web. This includes standardising indentation of paragraphs, standardising fonts and font size, converting images to a format that a web browser can properly display and adjusting tables, so they appear sensibly on a wide range of screen sizes and orientations.

Creating digital surrogates

  1. The National Archives anticipates that there may be several publication versions of the same judgment sent to us by the court over time.
  2. The National Archives will keep the digital record of each of the versions that are sent to us, but only publish the latest version authorised by the court.
  3. Amendments to the text of the currently published judgment can only be made by the court sending another publication version of the judgment to the archive through TDR.

Due diligence checks

  1. The National Archives will undertake due diligence checks before publishing each judgment.
  2. The checks are to mitigate the risk that a judgment is sent in error by the court; and for The National Archives to comply with its obligations as a publisher, in relation to reporting restrictions.
  3. The checking process takes a risk-based approach to achieve the right balance between speed of publication and the depth and thoroughness of checking.
  4. As a result of the checking process, The National Archives will either
    1. Publish the judgment (using the digital surrogate that has been created); or,
    2. Ask for clarification or confirmation from the court.
  1. The judge has the final word on suitability for publication and The National Archives will always publish judgments having received confirmation from the clerk or judge.
  2. The initial assessment involves:
    1. Checking whether the judgment says it is “Approved” and has been “released to The National Archives” for publication.
    2. Reading the first few sentences of the judgment body to see if there is a restriction or condition on publication (i.e., reporting restrictions).
    3. Checking for any obvious errors in the judgment header (dates / neutral citations).
    4. Checking the whole html and pdf versions of the judgment against the Word original to check for any formatting errors.
  1. In the case that the judgment is subject to reporting restrictions we will make an overall assessment on whether the version of the judgment supplied complies with any notice requiring anonymisation or redaction, checking whether the reporting restriction has been consistently applied throughout the body of the judgment e.g., consistent anonymisation of parties.
  2. The National Archives will iteratively develop this checking process considering operational experience and feedback.
  3. The National Archives will provide reports on the operation of the checking process to the Ministry of Justice, HMCTS, and the Judiciary.

Timing of publication

  1. The National Archives understands that rapid publication of judgments is important for the users of its service. Most judgments will be published within 2 working days of receipt. Judgments that have not appeared on the service after 2 working days of public hand-down, are likely to have not been sent to The National Archives for publication.
  2. Judgments with reporting restrictions may take longer, due to the checks required and whether we have requested confirmation or clarification from the court.
  3. The National Archives will measure the time it takes from receipt to publication and report on the results, including the reasons for delays.

Withdrawal from publication

  1. Once a judgment has been published, The National Archives will only withdraw it from publication if we are instructed to do so by the Court or Tribunal
  2. When the National Archives receives takedown requests from the public, the usual response will be to deny the request and refer them to the court/tribunal.
